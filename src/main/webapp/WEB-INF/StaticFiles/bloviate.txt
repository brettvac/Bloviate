Noun. Nouns designate persons, places, things or abstract qualities. They can stand on their own within sentences. “dog” - a noun, as can be seen from the meaning, or from the fact that it can be pluralized or rendered in the possessive: “a dog’s house”. Its status is one of countable noun, as can be seen from the fact that it can be used with the definite article, and cannot stand alone as a bare noun: *”Dog is here.”. “Luck”, on the other hand, is a non-countable noun. It cannot be pluralized, but it can take a definite article for a generic reference: “The luck of the Irish.”
Adjective. Adjectives designate the attributes that may attach to a particular noun. They may be used either attributively or predicatively within the sentence. Adjectives are recursive, and the only limit on the number of them that may occur lies in the ability of the mind to process the information. They are normally found after the determiners and before the head noun. Adjectives can be used predicatively after the copula, or after the verb to seem: “The woman is happy”, or “The woman seems happy.” Adjectives can be declined in the comparative or superlative degrees. Some adjectives, such as fond, take complements, as in “fond of sweets”. Such adjectives are not normally used attributively, although such use is possible: “He is visiting his out-of-work friends.” Other adjectives that frequently take complements are: envious of, proud of, good at, short of or cross with. Adjectives can be modified by adverbs. In addition, it may be observed that adjectives come in different sorts: adjectives that describe a property, such as beautiful woman, or adjectives that qualify a property already inherent in a noun, such as genuine genius or ersatz hero. Most often, this type of adjective tends to function as an intensifier. Some adjectives place a restriction on the references, such as his only friend, or his main argument.
Determiner. Among the categories of elements that precede nouns are the determiners. Central determiners include definite and indefinite articles, possessive adjectives, demonstratives, some relative pronouns such as what, which and whose, along with definite adjectives like few, several, every, each, no, the dual forms either and neither, some or any. The definite article is used when it is clear what is being referred to, whereas an indefinite article is used when a new entity is being introduced into the conversation, and this new entity is singular. (When plural unknowns are added into the conversation, they remain bare.). Whereas possessive adjectives do not have to agree with the head noun in number, demonstratives do. The indefinite adjectives function as quantifying determiners.
Possessive. Possessives come in two varieties: the inflexional possessive and the prepositional possessive. Sometimes they can be used interchangeably, but most often this is not the case. The descriptive can of beans, for instance, can barely be considered a possessive, and certainly cannot be rendered as *bean’s can. Within the noun phrase, the inflexion is always attached to the last noun or element in the phrase: “the Queen of England’s socks”.
Pronoun. Pronouns are words that take the place of nouns or noun phrases to avoid repetition. They can refer to people, places, things, or ideas previously mentioned or understood from context. Personal pronouns include I, you, he, she, it, we, they, and their object forms me, him, her, us, them. Possessive pronouns show ownership, such as mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs. Reflexive pronouns like myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves refer back to the subject. Demonstrative pronouns this, that, these, those point to specific things. Interrogative pronouns who, whom, whose, what, which are used in questions. Relative pronouns who, whom, whose, which, that introduce relative clauses. Indefinite pronouns such as someone, anyone, everybody, nothing, several, few refer to nonspecific persons or things.
Conjunction. Conjunctions are words that connect words, phrases, clauses, or sentences. Coordinating conjunctions such as and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet join elements of equal grammatical rank. Correlative conjunctions work in pairs like either…or, neither…nor, both…and, not only…but also. Subordinating conjunctions such as because, although, if, when, since, while, whereas introduce dependent clauses and show relationships like cause, time, condition, or contrast.
Interjection. Interjections are words or short expressions used to convey emotion, reaction, or exclamation. They often stand alone or are inserted into sentences and are usually followed by an exclamation mark. Common examples include oh!, wow!, ouch!, hey!, alas!, bravo!, damn!, and yikes!. They express surprise, pain, joy, greeting, hesitation, or other feelings and do not usually have a grammatical connection to the rest of the sentence.
Verb. Verbs convey the action, occurrence, or state of being within a sentence. They are the core of the predicate and can express tense, mood, voice, aspect, and person. Action verbs describe physical or mental actions such as run, think, write, or decide. Linking verbs like be, seem, appear, become, remain connect the subject to additional information about the subject. Auxiliary verbs such as have, be, do, will, can, may, must, should help form tenses, questions, negatives, or passive voice. Modal verbs express possibility, ability, permission, obligation, or necessity. Verbs can be transitive requiring a direct object, intransitive needing no object, or ditransitive taking both direct and indirect objects. Regular verbs form past tense and past participle by adding -ed, while irregular verbs follow unpredictable patterns such as go-went-gone or sing-sang-sung.
Adverb. Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, other adverbs, or entire sentences, often answering how, when, where, why, to what extent, or under what condition. Many adverbs end in -ly such as quickly, carefully, happily, but others do not like fast, hard, well, soon, here, there, very, quite. Adverbs of manner describe how an action is performed: “She sings beautifully.” Adverbs of time indicate when: “They arrived yesterday.” Adverbs of place show where: “The children are playing outside.” Adverbs of frequency tell how often: “He rarely complains.” Adverbs of degree express intensity: “It is extremely cold.” Sentence adverbs like fortunately, honestly, perhaps modify the whole statement: “Fortunately, no one was hurt.” Adverbs can be compared: more quickly, most quickly, or irregularly: well-better-best.
Article. Articles are a small class of determiners that specify the definiteness of a noun. The definite article “the” is used when the noun refers to something specific or previously mentioned: “The book on the table is mine.” The indefinite articles “a” and “an” introduce a non-specific singular countable noun: “I saw a cat” or “She ate an apple.” “An” is used before vowel sounds and “a” before consonant sounds. Articles do not appear with most proper nouns, plural non-specific nouns, or uncountable nouns when used generically: “Cats are independent” or “Water is essential.” Zero article occurs in cases like “Love conquers all” or “She studies law.”
Preposition. Prepositions are words that show the relationship between a noun or pronoun and other words in a sentence, often indicating direction, location, time, manner, cause, or possession. Common prepositions include in, on, at, by, with, from, to, for, of, about, under, over, between, among, through, across, during, before, after, since, until. They typically head prepositional phrases that function as adjectives or adverbs: “the book on the table” (location), “She arrived after lunch” (time), “He spoke with confidence” (manner). Some prepositions can function as adverbs when no object follows: “She looked up.” Compound prepositions such as because of, instead of, out of, in front of, on behalf of consist of more than one word. Prepositions often combine with verbs to form phrasal verbs that change meaning: “give up,” “look after,” “run into.”
Restrictive Adjective. Restrictive adjectives (also called limiting adjectives) place a restriction on the reference of the noun they modify, narrowing down the possible things referred to rather than describing a quality. They include words like only, main, chief, principal, sole, primary, exact, very (in the sense of precise), same, other, certain, particular, next, last, former, latter. Examples: “his only child” (restricts to one specific child), “the main argument” (specifies the primary one among others), “the very book I wanted” (pinpoints exactly that one). Unlike descriptive adjectives, restrictive adjectives do not usually accept degrees of comparison and serve to identify or delimit rather than attribute a property.
Regular Adjective. Regular adjectives are the typical descriptive adjectives that express qualities, states, or characteristics of nouns, such as size, color, shape, age, origin, material, or opinion. They include words like big, small, red, beautiful, happy, old, French, wooden, interesting, difficult. They can normally be used both attributively (“a beautiful garden”) and predicatively (“the garden is beautiful”). Most regular adjectives can form comparatives and superlatives (bigger/biggest, more beautiful/most beautiful), can be modified by adverbs (“very happy”), and follow the usual order when multiple adjectives appear before a noun (opinion-size-age-shape-color-origin-material-purpose + noun).
Nominal Adjective. Nominal adjectives (also called adjective substantives or substantivized adjectives) function as nouns while retaining an adjectival meaning; they stand alone without a head noun and take on noun roles such as subject, object, or complement. They often refer to people or things characterized by the quality named by the adjective. Examples: “the rich” (meaning rich people), “the poor help the needy”, “the beautiful is not always good”, “He prefers the red to the blue”, “the French love wine” (the French people), “the impossible became possible”. Many nominal adjectives use “the” to indicate a class of people (“the elderly”, “the young”, “the injured”) or abstract qualities (“the unknown”, “the absurd”). Some can take plural endings when referring to individuals (“the blacks”, “the whites” in certain contexts), while others remain invariable.
Predeterminer. Pre-determiners (all, both, half, one third), as well as intensifiers (such as what and such) may precede a determiner, and so must be considered a separate class. This is so because determiners are non-recursive, meaning that there cannot be more than one in use at a time. However, one predeterminer may be used along with one determiner. No *both all or *this the. All predeterminers involve notions of quantity. The test of whether or not an item is a predeterminer is found in whether or not it can occur before an article or demonstrative. “All the boys”, “Half the mayonnaise”, etc. In cases involving the intensifiers what and such, note must be taken of a peculiarity: sentences containing what can be “fronted” (the intensifier moved to the front of the sentence), whereas sentences containing such cannot. “What a wonderful cook he is!”, but “He’s such a wonderful cook!”. As well, sentences in the negative must be constructed with such, and are therefore ineligible for fronting: “He’s not such a great cook!”
Postdeterminer. Post-determiners follow central determiners in the noun phrase and provide additional specification, usually involving quantity, order, or cardinality. They include cardinal numerals (one, two, three), ordinal numerals (first, second, third), quantifying words (many, few, several, little, much, enough, more, most, other, another, certain, various, numerous), and some quantifying adjectives like same, next, last, former, latter, usual, typical. Examples: “the three books”, “these many people”, “my first attempt”, “the other students”, “those few remaining tickets”, “all the other possibilities”. Post-determiners can combine in specific orders (e.g., cardinal + ordinal: “the first two days”, or quantifier + cardinal: “the many thousands of stars”), but they follow the central determiner and precede any adjectives. Unlike central determiners, post-determiners are recursive to some extent, allowing sequences like “these first few attempts”.
Static Verb. Static verbs (also called stative verbs) describe states, conditions, situations, or unchanging properties rather than actions or processes; they typically do not occur in continuous/progressive tenses because they represent something inherent or permanent. Common examples include be, seem, appear, exist, belong, own, possess, contain, include, consist of, know, understand, believe, think (in the sense of opinion), remember, forget, like, love, hate, prefer, want, need, deserve, matter, cost, weigh. Examples: “She knows the answer” (not *She is knowing the answer), “This book belongs to me”, “The soup tastes delicious”, “He owns three cars”. Some verbs can be both static and dynamic depending on meaning: “think” as opinion (“I think it’s true”) vs. process (“I’m thinking about it”).
Transitive Verb. Transitive verbs require a direct object to complete their meaning; they express an action that passes from the subject to something or someone else. The direct object answers “what?” or “whom?” after the verb. Examples: “She kicked the ball” (ball is the direct object), “They built a house”, “He read the book”, “We invited her”. Many transitive verbs can also take indirect objects: “She gave him a gift” (him = indirect, gift = direct). Some verbs are strictly transitive and cannot stand without an object: “She resembles her mother” (not *She resembles). Others can be transitive or intransitive depending on context: “He runs the company” (transitive) vs. “He runs every morning” (intransitive).
Stative Verb. Stative verbs (also known as state verbs or static verbs) express a state, condition, feeling, thought, possession, or sensory perception rather than an action or event in progress; they are generally not used in continuous tenses. Examples include verbs of perception (see, hear, smell, taste, feel), mental states (know, believe, understand, remember, think, suppose), emotions (love, hate, like, prefer, want), possession (have, own, belong, possess), and being (be, seem, appear, exist). Examples: “I love chocolate” (not usually *I am loving chocolate), “This house belongs to my parents”, “She understands the problem”. Some stative verbs can shift to dynamic use with a change in meaning: “have” as possession (“I have a car”) vs. action (“I’m having lunch”).
Dynamic Verb. Dynamic verbs (also called action verbs) describe physical actions, processes, changes, or activities that can begin, continue, and end; they can normally be used in continuous/progressive tenses to show ongoing action. Examples include run, walk, jump, eat, drink, write, read, speak, listen, work, play, dance, grow, change, become, arrive, leave, hit, kick, build, paint. Examples: “She is running in the park”, “They were eating dinner”, “He has been writing a novel”. Dynamic verbs contrast with stative verbs and include both transitive and intransitive types. Some verbs can function dynamically or statically depending on context: “look” as action (“She is looking at the picture”) vs. state (“She looks tired”).
Verb Aspect. Verb aspect indicates the internal temporal structure of an action or state—how it unfolds over time—independent of tense. The main aspects in English are simple (complete or habitual), progressive/continuous (ongoing at a specific time), perfect (completed with relevance to another time), and perfect progressive (ongoing up to a point with relevance). Examples: simple aspect “She walks to school” (habit), progressive “She is walking to school” (happening now), perfect “She has walked to school” (completed action with present relevance), perfect progressive “She has been walking for an hour” (duration up to now). Aspect combines with tense to form complex verb phrases: “She had been walking” (past perfect progressive).
Main Clause. A main clause (also called an independent clause) is a clause that contains a subject and a finite verb and expresses a complete thought; it can stand alone as a complete sentence. It does not depend on any other clause for its meaning. Examples: “She laughed”, “The dog chased the cat”, “We will arrive tomorrow”. Main clauses can be joined by coordinating conjunctions to form compound sentences: “She laughed and he smiled”. Every simple sentence consists of one main clause, and complex or compound-complex sentences contain at least one main clause along with subordinate clauses.
Subordinate Clause. A subordinate clause (also called a dependent clause) contains a subject and a verb but does not express a complete thought and cannot stand alone as a sentence; it depends on a main clause to complete its meaning. Subordinate clauses are introduced by subordinating conjunctions (because, although, if, when, since, while, unless, until, whereas), relative pronouns (who, whom, whose, which, that), or other subordinators. Types include adverbial clauses (“She stayed because it rained”), relative clauses (“The book that I read was fascinating”), and noun clauses (“I know what you did”). Examples: “Although it was late, we continued working” (subordinate clause: Although it was late), “He left before the movie ended”.
Phrase. A phrase is a group of words that functions as a single unit within a sentence but does not contain both a subject and a finite verb, so it cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. Phrases act as modifiers or complements and are named after their head word or main function. Noun phrases (“the old wooden chair”), verb phrases (“has been running quickly”), adjective phrases (“very proud of her achievement”), adverb phrases (“with great care”), prepositional phrases (“in the morning mist”). A phrase can be simple (one word) or complex (multiple words with modifiers). Phrases expand the meaning of words and provide detail without forming independent clauses.
Clause. A clause is a group of words containing a subject and a predicate (finite verb) that expresses some idea. Clauses are either main (independent) or subordinate (dependent). Main clauses can stand alone as sentences: “The sun rises in the east.” Subordinate clauses cannot stand alone and depend on a main clause for complete meaning: “when the sun rises” (needs a main clause like “Birds sing when the sun rises”). Clauses are classified by function: noun clauses (“What she said surprised me”), adverbial clauses (“He left because it was late”), relative (adjectival) clauses (“The man who called is my brother”). Multiple clauses combine to form compound, complex, or compound-complex sentences.
Gerund. A gerund is the -ing form of a verb that functions as a noun. It can act as subject, object, complement, or object of a preposition. Examples: “Swimming is good exercise” (subject), “I enjoy reading novels” (direct object), “Her favorite hobby is painting” (subject complement), “He succeeded by working hard” (object of preposition). Gerunds can take objects and be modified by adverbs: “Running marathons quickly requires training.” Unlike present participles (which function as adjectives or parts of verb phrases), gerunds always serve nominal roles. Possessive pronouns often precede gerunds in formal usage: “I appreciate your helping me.”
Participle. A participle is a verb form that functions as an adjective or as part of a verb phrase. English has two main participles: present participle (ending in -ing: running, singing, being) and past participle (regular verbs end in -ed: walked, painted; irregular forms vary: gone, written, broken). Present participles describe ongoing action or state: “The barking dog woke me.” Past participles describe completed action or passive state: “The broken window needs repair.” Participles head participial phrases: “Running through the park, she felt free.” When used in verb tenses, participles combine with auxiliaries: “She is singing,” “They have finished.” Dangling participles occur when the participle’s implied subject does not match the main clause subject: *“Walking home, the rain started.” (corrected: “Walking home, she got caught in the rain.”)
Infinitive. The infinitive is the base form of a verb, usually preceded by “to” (to run, to eat, to be), though the bare infinitive omits “to” after certain verbs. It can function as a noun (“To err is human”), adjective (“a book to read”), or adverb (“She came to help”). Infinitives appear after verbs like want, need, decide, plan, hope, promise, agree, refuse, learn, offer (“I want to leave”), after adjectives (“easy to understand”), and in purpose clauses (“She studied hard to pass the exam”). Split infinitives insert an adverb between “to” and the verb (“to boldly go”); they are now widely accepted. Bare infinitives follow modal verbs (“She can swim”), verbs of perception (“I saw him run”), and causatives like make, let, have (“They made us wait”).
Passive Voice. Passive voice is a construction where the subject receives the action rather than performing it; the doer (agent) may be omitted or introduced with “by”. It is formed with a form of “be” + past participle: “The cake was eaten” (simple past passive), “The report is being reviewed” (present progressive passive), “The house has been painted” (present perfect passive). Passive is used when the agent is unknown, unimportant, obvious, or to focus on the action/receiver: “Mistakes were made”, “The president was elected in 2020”, “The law should be obeyed”. Only transitive verbs can form passives. Active and passive can often be interchanged: “The chef cooked the meal” → “The meal was cooked by the chef.”
Modal Verb. Modal verbs express necessity, possibility, permission, ability, obligation, advice, or prediction; they are a special class of auxiliary verbs that do not change form for person or number and are followed by the bare infinitive. The core modals are can, could, may, might, will, would, shall, should, must; semi-modals include ought to, need to, dare, used to. Examples: “She can swim” (ability), “You must finish this” (obligation), “It might rain” (possibility), “May I leave?” (permission), “He should see a doctor” (advice). Modals do not take -s in third person, do not use “to” before the main verb, and form negatives/questions without “do”: “She cannot come”, “Will you help?”.
Relative Clause. A relative clause (also called adjective clause) is a dependent clause that modifies a noun or pronoun (the antecedent) and is introduced by a relative pronoun (who, whom, whose, which, that) or relative adverb (when, where, why). Defining (restrictive) relative clauses provide essential information and are not set off by commas: “The man who called yesterday is here.” Non-defining (non-restrictive) relative clauses add extra, non-essential information and are set off by commas: “My brother, who lives in Toronto, is visiting.” “That” is often used for defining clauses with people or things, while “which” is preferred for non-defining. Reduced relative clauses omit the pronoun and “be”: “The book written by her won an award” (from “which was written by her”).
Existential There. There exists a non-literal or existential use of “there”, as in: “There are rabbits in the hall.” This phrase does not describe the location of a gang of rabbits previously known to exist, but merely affirms that these rabbits do in fact exist. The literal or locative use of “there” as in “John’s there in the hall” refers to the current location of a previously identified individual. However, note should be taken of the following: “There are the rabbits in the hall.” In this case, the use is literal, as it contains the definite article, which always signals a literal use. If a contradictory adverb is also present in the sentence, then the “there” has to be existential: “There really are rabbits in the hall.” (Stress will also be laid upon the verb in this instance. Additionally, the fact that “in the hall” is a true locative rules out the notion that the “there” could also be used locatively in this case, as only one true locative may be present in any phrase). In all cases, the existential use of there is constructed through the use of the copula. It always conveys an abstract notion.
Inversion. Inversion is the reversal of the normal subject-verb order in a sentence, often for emphasis, literary effect, question formation, or after certain adverbs or phrases. Subject-verb inversion occurs in questions (“Is she coming?”), after negative or restrictive adverbs/phrases (“Never have I seen such beauty”, “Only then did he realize”), in conditional clauses without “if” (“Had I known, I would have helped”), and in literary or aphoristic statements (“Happy is the man who listens well”). In direct speech reporting, inversion is common after verbs of saying or thinking: “I need to make my dinner,” thought Mary. “I need to go grocery shopping,” said John to Bill. However, when indirect speech is involved, inversion is not employed: “John said his fridge was almost empty.” Inversion can also appear in poetic or formal styles to place emphasis on the complement or adverbial element.
Noun Phrase. A noun phrase is a phrase with a noun or pronoun as its head word, functioning as a single unit in the sentence, typically as subject, object, complement, or object of a preposition. It consists of the head noun plus optional modifiers such as determiners, adjectives, prepositional phrases, or relative clauses. Noun phrases can be simple (“the dog”) or complex (“the big brown dog that chased the cat yesterday”). They allow detailed description without forming clauses. Examples: “A tall glass of cold water” (subject). “She bought fresh vegetables from the market” (object). “The president of the company announced the merger” (subject). “His decision to leave surprised everyone” (subject with infinitive phrase). “The house with the red door belongs to my uncle” (subject with prepositional phrase). “What you said hurt her feelings” (noun clause acting as subject). Noun phrases exhibit number agreement with verbs and can take possessives or appositives.
Verb Phrase. A verb phrase consists of a main verb plus its auxiliaries, modals, or particles, functioning as the predicate of a clause and conveying tense, aspect, mood, and voice. It includes the head verb and any helping verbs or adverbs that modify it. Verb phrases can be simple (“runs”) or complex (“has been running quickly”). Examples: “She is singing beautifully tonight” (present progressive). “They will have finished by tomorrow” (future perfect). “He might be coming later” (modal + progressive). “The letter was written yesterday” (passive voice). “We should have been studying harder” (modal + perfect progressive). Verb phrases carry the primary temporal and aspectual information of the sentence and can include negation (“did not go”) or questions (“Did she go?”).
Adjective Phrase. An adjective phrase is a phrase headed by an adjective that modifies a noun or pronoun, providing descriptive detail; it can include adverbs, prepositional phrases, or complements modifying the adjective. Adjective phrases function attributively before the noun or predicatively after linking verbs. Examples: “Extremely proud of her son” (predicative). “A remarkably intelligent student” (attributive). “Happy to help you” (with infinitive complement). “Afraid of the dark” (with prepositional complement). “Quite satisfied with the results” (with adverb and prepositional phrase). “More beautiful than ever” (comparative with than-clause). Adjective phrases allow nuanced qualification and often follow the adjective order rules when multiple occur.
Adverb Phrase. An adverb phrase is a phrase headed by an adverb that modifies a verb, adjective, another adverb, or clause, indicating manner, time, place, frequency, degree, or other circumstances. It can include intensifiers or prepositional elements. Examples: “With great enthusiasm” (manner). “Very carefully indeed” (degree with intensifiers). “In the early morning” (time, functioning adverbially). “Quite slowly at first” (manner and degree). “Right here on the table” (place). “More frequently than expected” (frequency with comparative). Adverb phrases provide flexibility in placement (initial, medial, final) and add precision to modification without requiring a full clause.
Prepositional Phrase. A prepositional phrase consists of a preposition followed by its object (usually a noun phrase or pronoun) and any modifiers; it functions as an adjective (modifying a noun) or adverb (modifying a verb, adjective, or clause), showing relationships like location, time, direction, manner, cause, or possession. Examples: “Under the old oak tree” (location, adverbial). “With considerable effort” (manner, adverbial). “Of great importance” (quality, adjectival modifying noun). “After the storm passed” (time, adverbial). “Between you and me” (relationship). “Because of the rain” (cause). Prepositional phrases are highly versatile, often movable in sentences, and form the basis of many idioms and phrasal verbs when combined with verbs.
Participial Phrase. A participial phrase is a phrase headed by a participle (present -ing or past -ed/en) that functions as an adjective modifying a noun or pronoun; it includes the participle, its modifiers, objects, or complements. Participial phrases provide descriptive detail and often indicate simultaneous or prior action. Examples: “Running through the rain, she got soaked” (present participial phrase modifying she). “Exhausted by the long journey, the travelers rested” (past participial phrase modifying travelers). “The child crying loudly needed attention” (present, modifying child). “Books written in the 19th century often use complex language” (past, modifying books). “Smiling broadly, he accepted the award” (present, adverbial-like modification). Care must be taken to avoid dangling participles where the modified noun is unclear.
Infinitive Phrase. An infinitive phrase consists of the infinitive verb (to + base form or bare) plus its objects, complements, or modifiers; it can function as noun, adjective, or adverb. Infinitive phrases express purpose, intention, or result. Examples: “To win the championship requires dedication” (noun, subject). “She has a novel to finish by Friday” (adjective modifying novel). “He came to apologize for his mistake” (adverbial of purpose). “To speak honestly, I disagree” (sentence modifier). “They want us to leave early” (object of want). “The best thing to do is wait” (noun complement). Infinitive phrases are versatile and common after verbs of wanting, planning, or perception.
Gerund Phrase. A gerund phrase is headed by a gerund (-ing verb form functioning as noun) plus its objects, modifiers, or complements; it acts as subject, object, complement, or object of preposition. Gerund phrases nominalize actions. Examples: “Swimming in the ocean relaxes me” (subject). “I enjoy playing chess on weekends” (direct object). “Her habit of arriving late annoys everyone” (object of preposition). “Winning the lottery changed his life” (subject). “By studying diligently, she passed the exam” (object of preposition). “The hardest part is getting started” (subject complement). Gerund phrases often take possessive modifiers in formal style: “I appreciate your helping me.”
Appositive Phrase. An appositive phrase is a noun phrase that renames or explains another noun or pronoun right beside it, usually set off by commas for non-restrictive cases or without commas for restrictive ones. Appositives provide additional identification or description. Examples: “My brother, an excellent chef, cooked dinner” (non-restrictive appositive). “The capital city Paris attracts millions of tourists” (restrictive, no commas). “Einstein, the famous physicist, developed relativity” (non-restrictive). “Her dog Rover barked loudly” (restrictive). “The director himself approved the script” (intensive appositive with pronoun). Appositive phrases add clarity or emphasis and can be reduced from relative clauses.
Absolute Phrase. An absolute phrase (also called nominative absolute) is a phrase consisting of a noun or pronoun plus a participle (often with modifiers), functioning independently to provide additional information about the whole sentence; it is not grammatically connected to the main clause and is set off by commas. Absolute phrases express attendant circumstances. Examples: “Weather permitting, we will go hiking” (absolute phrase indicating condition). “His work finished, he went home” (indicating completion). “The storm over, calm returned” (indicating prior event). “Eyes wide open, she stared in amazement” (describing manner). “All things considered, the plan succeeded” (considering circumstances). Absolute phrases add sophistication and are common in formal or literary writing.
Noun Clause. A noun clause is a dependent clause that functions as a noun within the sentence, serving as subject, object, complement, object of preposition, or appositive. It is typically introduced by words like that, what, who, whether, how, why, if, or question words (who, what, where, when, why, how). Noun clauses express ideas that can stand in for nouns. Examples: “What she said surprised everyone” (subject). “I believe that he is honest” (object of believe). “The problem is whether we can finish on time” (subject complement). “He told me why he left early” (direct object). “Your success depends on how hard you work” (object of preposition on). “The fact that it rained ruined the picnic” (appositive to fact). Noun clauses often begin with “that” (sometimes omitted in informal speech: “I know you’re right”).
Adverbial Clause. An adverbial clause (also called adverb clause) is a dependent clause that functions as an adverb, modifying a verb, adjective, another adverb, or the entire main clause, answering questions like how, when, where, why, to what extent, under what condition, or purpose. It is introduced by subordinating conjunctions such as because, although, if, when, since, while, until, before, after, as, whereas, unless, provided that, so that. Examples: “She left because it was late” (reason). “Although it was raining, we went out” (concession). “He’ll call when he arrives” (time). “If you study, you will pass” (condition). “They worked so that they could finish early” (purpose). “Wherever she goes, trouble follows” (place). Adverbial clauses are movable and often set off by commas when they begin the sentence.
Adjectival Phrase. An adjectival phrase is any phrase (not necessarily headed by an adjective) that functions as an adjective modifying a noun or pronoun; common types include prepositional phrases, participial phrases, and infinitive phrases acting adjectivally. Adjectival phrases provide descriptive detail equivalent to single adjectives. Examples: “The man in the black coat left” (prepositional phrase modifying man). “The letter written yesterday arrived today” (participial phrase modifying letter). “A place to relax after work” (infinitive phrase modifying place). “The book on the top shelf” (prepositional phrase modifying book). “Children tired from playing fell asleep” (participial phrase modifying children). Adjectival phrases expand noun modification beyond simple adjectives and follow typical adjective placement rules.
Conditional Clause. A conditional clause is a type of adverbial clause that expresses a condition under which the main clause action occurs; it is introduced by subordinating conjunctions such as if, unless, provided (that), supposing (that), on condition that, as long as, in case, or inverted structures without if (e.g., should, were, had). Conditional clauses form the basis of conditional sentences (zero, first, second, third, mixed). Examples: “If it rains, we will stay inside” (first conditional). “Had I known, I would have helped” (third conditional inversion). “Unless you hurry, you’ll miss the train” (negative condition). “Provided that funds are available, the project will proceed” (formal condition). “Should you need assistance, call me” (inversion for first conditional). Conditional clauses often appear initially (with comma) or finally (no comma).
Concessive Clause. A concessive clause is an adverbial clause that expresses contrast or concession, indicating that something happens despite an opposing condition; it is introduced by subordinating conjunctions although, though, even though, whereas, while, even if, despite the fact that. Concessive clauses highlight unexpected outcomes. Examples: “Although he was tired, he kept working” (concession). “Even though it was expensive, she bought it” (strong contrast). “While I disagree, I respect your opinion” (mild concession). “Even if it rains, the event will continue” (hypothetical concession). “Whereas some prefer tea, others like coffee” (formal contrast). Concessive clauses are often fronted for emphasis and separated by commas.
Purpose Clause. A purpose clause is an adverbial clause that expresses the intention or aim of the action in the main clause; it is commonly introduced by so that, in order that, or (in order) to + infinitive (reduced form), less commonly lest. Purpose clauses answer “why” or “for what purpose.” Examples: “She studied hard so that she could pass the exam” (purpose). “He whispered in order that no one would hear” (formal purpose). “We left early to avoid traffic” (reduced infinitive purpose clause). “Speak clearly lest there be misunderstandings” (negative purpose, archaic/formal). “They saved money so they could travel” (purpose with so). Purpose clauses often follow the main clause but can be fronted for emphasis.
Result Clause. A result clause is an adverbial clause that expresses the outcome or consequence of the situation in the main clause; it is typically introduced by so…that, such…that, or occasionally that alone in formal contexts. Result clauses show cause-effect relationships. Examples: “It was so cold that the lake froze” (result). “He spoke such nonsense that everyone laughed” (result with such). “The music was loud so that we couldn’t talk” (informal result). “It happened so quickly that no one reacted” (speed of result). “She was such a good teacher that students loved her classes” (result). Result clauses follow the main clause and emphasize degree leading to outcome.
Temporal Clause. A temporal clause is an adverbial clause that indicates the time of the main clause action; it is introduced by subordinating conjunctions when, while, as, before, after, since, until, till, as soon as, once, whenever, the moment (that). Temporal clauses establish sequence or simultaneity. Examples: “When the bell rings, class ends” (time). “While she was cooking, the phone rang” (simultaneous action). “After he finished, he left” (sequence). “As soon as I arrive, I’ll call you” (immediate time). “Since we moved here, life has improved” (time from a point). “Until you apologize, I won’t speak to you” (limit of time). Temporal clauses are frequently fronted with a comma for clarity.
Relative Adverb Clause. A relative adverb clause is a type of relative clause introduced by a relative adverb (where, when, why, whereby, wherein, whereupon) rather than a relative pronoun; it modifies a noun of place, time, or reason and functions adjectivally. Relative adverb clauses provide essential or additional information about the antecedent. Examples: “This is the house where I grew up” (where modifying house, place). “I remember the day when we first met” (when modifying day, time). “That’s the reason why she left” (why modifying reason). “The office wherein the meeting was held is upstairs” (wherein modifying office, formal place). “The moment whereupon he arrived chaos ensued” (whereupon modifying moment, sequence). Relative adverb clauses can sometimes be reduced or rephrased with prepositions plus which/that.
Reduced Relative Clause. A reduced relative clause is a shortened form of a relative clause where the relative pronoun and a form of “be” are omitted, leaving a participial phrase, prepositional phrase, or other structure that still modifies the antecedent noun. Reduction is common in defining relative clauses for conciseness. Examples: “The man (who is) standing there is my uncle” → “The man standing there is my uncle” (present participle). “The book (which was) written by Austen is classic” → “The book written by Austen is classic” (past participle). “Anyone (who is) interested should apply” → “Anyone interested should apply”. “The car (that is) in the driveway needs washing” → “The car in the driveway needs washing” (prepositional phrase). Reduced relative clauses maintain the restrictive/non-restrictive distinction of their full forms.
Coordinate Clause. A coordinate clause is one of two or more main (independent) clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet) or a semicolon, forming a compound sentence of equal grammatical rank. Coordinate clauses express related but independent ideas. Examples: “She wanted to go but it was raining” (contrast with but). “He studied hard and he passed the exam” (addition with and). “You can come with us or you can stay home” (alternative with or). “It was late so we went to bed” (result with so). “I was tired yet I kept working” (concession with yet). Coordinate clauses can be linked with correlative conjunctions too: “Not only did she sing but she also danced”.
Elliptical Clause. An elliptical clause is a clause in which words are omitted but understood from context, often for conciseness or parallelism; the omitted elements are recoverable from the surrounding sentence or prior clause. Elliptical clauses commonly appear in comparisons, responses, or coordinated structures. Examples: “She is taller than I (am)” (omitted verb in comparative). “He can run faster than she (can run)” (elliptical comparative clause). “When (it is) possible I’ll visit” (omitted subject and verb). “If (it is) necessary call me” (conditional ellipsis). “Some like coffee while others (like) tea” (parallel ellipsis). Elliptical clauses maintain grammatical balance and avoid unnecessary repetition.
Nominal Relative Clause. A nominal relative clause (also called free relative clause or fused relative clause) is a relative clause that functions as a noun phrase without an explicit antecedent; it is introduced by what, whatever, whoever, whichever, where, when, why, how and stands in place of a noun as subject, object, or complement. Nominal relative clauses are self-contained. Examples: “What you need is rest” (subject, equivalent to “the thing that you need”). “I’ll take whatever is available” (object). “Whoever wins gets the prize” (subject). “This is how it happened” (complement). “Wherever she goes she causes excitement” (adverbial but nominal in some analyses). “Give it to whoever asks first” (object of preposition). Nominal relatives often convey indefinite or general reference.
Comment Clause. A comment clause is a parenthetical clause that expresses the speaker’s attitude, opinion, or evaluation toward the main clause; it is inserted into or added to the sentence and set off by commas, dashes, or parentheses. Comment clauses are often short and formulaic. Examples: “The plan I believe will succeed” (opinion). “Fortunately the weather improved” (evaluation). “To be honest I don’t know” (frankness). “You know what I mean” (shared understanding). “As you can see it’s quite simple” (demonstration). “The results surprisingly were positive” (surprise). Comment clauses add interpersonal nuance and are common in spoken and informal written English.
Cleft Clause. A cleft clause (or it-cleft) is a construction that splits a simple sentence into two clauses for emphasis using “it” + be + focused element + that/who/which clause; it highlights a particular part of the information. Cleft sentences focus attention on the emphasized element. Examples: “It was John who called yesterday” (focus on John). “It is tomorrow that we leave” (focus on time). “It was because of the rain that we stayed” (focus on reason). “It was in Paris where they met” (focus on place). “What surprised me was her decision” (wh-cleft variant). Cleft constructions are useful for contrast or clarification in discourse.
Pseudo-Cleft Clause. A pseudo-cleft clause (or wh-cleft) is a construction that divides information into a wh-clause (what, who, where, when, why, how) acting as subject or complement and a focused element linked by be; it emphasizes the focused part and often restructures for topic-comment clarity. Pseudo-clefts are reversible. Examples: “What I need is coffee” (focus on coffee). “What happened was unexpected” (focus on unexpected). “Who broke the vase was the cat” (focus on the cat). “The thing that worries me is the cost” (variant). “Where we went wrong was in planning” (focus on planning). Pseudo-clefts provide thematic prominence and are common in explanatory or corrective speech.
Comparative Clause. A comparative clause is a type of adverbial or adjectival clause introduced by than or as that expresses comparison of degree quantity or manner typically following a comparative adjective adverb or structure like as…as more…than less…than Comparative clauses complete the comparison and often involve ellipsis.Examples:“She runs faster than he does” (faster than he does comparative clause).“This book is as interesting as that one is” (as…as comparative).“He speaks more fluently than I expected” (more…than).“It cost less than we thought it would” (less…than with ellipsis).“The task was harder than it seemed at first” (harder than).Comparative clauses frequently omit repeated elements for conciseness and maintain parallelism in structure.
Equative Clause. An equative clause is an adverbial clause of comparison using as…as to indicate equality of degree or manner typically following an adjective or adverb in the positive form.Equative clauses establish similarity or equivalence.Examples:“She is as tall as her brother is” (as tall as).“He works as hard as anyone else does” (as hard as).“The movie was as good as I hoped it would be” (as good as).“They arrived as early as possible” (as…as with possible).“It isn’t as simple as it looks” (negative equative).Equative clauses often appear with ellipsis of the verb when context is clear:“She sings as beautifully as her teacher.”
Manner Clause. A manner clause is an adverbial clause that describes how the action of the main clause is performed introduced by subordinating conjunctions as though as if in the way that the way or sometimes how.Manner clauses answer the question “how?”Examples:“She acted as if nothing had happened” (as if manner).“Do it the way I showed you” (the way manner).“He speaks as though he knows everything” (as though hypothetical manner).“They behaved in the way that we expected” (in the way that).“Cook it how you like it best” (how informal manner).Manner clauses can express real or hypothetical manner and often use subjunctive-like forms after as if/as though.
Reason Clause. A reason clause is an adverbial clause that explains why the main clause action occurs introduced by subordinating conjunctions because since as for the reason that seeing that in that or on the grounds that.Reason clauses provide causal explanation.Examples:“We stayed inside because it was raining” (because reason).“Since you’re here you can help” (since reason).“As it was late we hurried home” (as reason).“He succeeded for the reason that he worked tirelessly” (formal reason).“She was upset in that her plans failed” (in that reason).Reason clauses are often interchangeable with because but since/as can imply known or given reasons.
Place Clause. A place clause is an adverbial clause indicating location or direction introduced by where wherever anywhere everywhere whence whither or combinations like to where.Place clauses answer “where?” and can indicate position or movement.Examples:“Stay where you are” (where place).“Go wherever you want” (wherever indefinite place).“I’ll meet you anywhere you choose” (anywhere indefinite).“They returned whence they came” (whence origin formal).“Follow the path whither it leads” (whither direction archaic).Place clauses are less common than other adverbial types but useful for spatial relationships.
Degree Clause. A degree clause is an adverbial clause that expresses the extent or intensity of an adjective adverb or verb often introduced by so…that such…that to the extent that or that alone in some constructions.Degree clauses indicate result from intensity.Examples:“It was so dark that we couldn’t see” (so…that degree/result).“He ran to the extent that he exhausted himself” (to the extent that).“She was such a fast runner that no one caught her” (such…that).“The noise was that loud we had to shout” (that informal degree).Degree clauses overlap with result clauses but focus more on the measure of quality leading to an outcome.
Conjunctive Adverb Clause. A conjunctive adverb clause is formed when a conjunctive adverb (however therefore moreover nevertheless consequently furthermore meanwhile) joins two independent clauses typically with a semicolon before and comma after creating a compound sentence with adverbial transition.Conjunctive adverb clauses link ideas logically.Examples:“It was raining;however we went anyway” (however contrast).“She studied hard;therefore she passed” (therefore result).“He was tired;nevertheless he continued” (nevertheless concession).“The plan failed;consequently we revised it” (consequently cause-effect).“They arrived late;meanwhile the meeting started” (meanwhile time).These structures provide smooth transitions and are common in formal writing.
Exclamatory Clause. An exclamatory clause is a type of independent clause that expresses strong emotion surprise emphasis or exclamation often beginning with what how or an interjection and typically ending with an exclamation mark.Exclamatory clauses function as complete sentences or stand alone for dramatic effect.Examples:“What a beautiful day it is!” (what exclamatory).“How quickly time passes!” (how exclamatory).“What nonsense you speak!” (emphasis on nonsense).“How clever you are!” (praise).“Oh what a tangled web we weave!” (literary exclamation).Exclamatory clauses can be reduced:“What a mess!” or inverted for style:“How strange the world seems!”
Imperative Clause.An imperative clause is an independent clause that issues a command request instruction suggestion or prohibition usually with the base form of the verb and an implied second-person subject (you).Imperative clauses often omit the subject and end with a period or exclamation mark depending on force.Examples:“Close the door.” (command).“Please sit down.” (polite request).“Don’t touch that!” (prohibition).“Let’s go for a walk.” (first-person plural suggestion).“Be careful on the ice.” (advice).Imperative clauses can include emphatic do:“Do be quiet.” or negative forms:“Don’t forget your keys.”
Interrogative Clause. An interrogative clause is a clause that asks a question and can function as an independent sentence (direct question) or embedded within a larger sentence (indirect question or embedded question).Direct interrogative clauses invert subject and auxiliary or use question words while indirect ones retain statement word order.Examples:“Are you coming?” (direct yes-no).“Where did she go?” (direct wh-question).“I wonder if he knows the truth.” (indirect yes-no).“She asked what time it was.” (indirect wh-question).“Tell me why you left.” (embedded imperative-like).Interrogative clauses use rising intonation in speech and question marks in writing for direct forms.
Negative Clause. A negative clause is any clause containing negation typically formed with not n't never no none nobody nothing nowhere no one or negative adverbs scarcely hardly barely.Negation can apply to the verb auxiliary or other elements affecting scope.Examples:“She does not like coffee.” (verb negation).“I have never been there.” (adverb negation).“Nobody knows the answer.” (indefinite pronoun negation).“We can’t go unless invited.” (modal negation).“Hardly had he arrived when it started raining.” (inversion with negative adverb).Negative clauses often trigger polarity items like any ever at all and can create double negatives in non-standard dialects:“I don’t know nothing.”
Affirmative Clause. An affirmative clause is a clause that expresses positive assertion without negation contrasting with negative clauses.Affirmative clauses use positive verb forms and avoid negative words unless in rhetorical contexts.Examples:“She likes tea.” (simple affirmative).“We will succeed.” (future affirmative).“He has finished the work.” (perfect affirmative).“They are happy.” (present affirmative).“Yes I do.” (emphatic affirmative response).Affirmative clauses can use do-support for emphasis:“I do believe you.” and serve as the unmarked form in most declarative sentences.
Declarative Clause. A declarative clause is an independent clause that makes a statement conveys information or expresses an assertion typically with subject-verb-object order and ending in a period.Declarative clauses are the most common sentence type in English.Examples:“The sun rises in the east.” (general truth).“She bought a new car yesterday.” (past event).“We are going to the beach tomorrow.” (future plan).“He thinks it’s a good idea.” (opinion).“Rain is forecast for later.” (prediction).Declarative clauses can be simple compound complex or compound-complex depending on clause structure.
Article. A definite or indefinite article is a type of determiner that specifies the definiteness of the following noun.The definite article the indicates a specific particular or previously mentioned noun while the indefinite articles a and an introduce a non-specific singular countable noun.Examples:“The moon is bright tonight.” (definite specific).“A dog barked in the distance.” (indefinite first mention).“An apple a day keeps the doctor away.” (indefinite generic).“The water in this glass is cold.” (definite mass noun).Articles do not appear with most proper nouns plural generics or uncountable nouns in general statements:“Cats love milk.” (zero article).“She studies law.” (zero article).The choice of a/an depends on pronunciation:a before consonant sounds an before vowel sounds.
Quantifier. Quantifiers are words or phrases that indicate quantity amount or number in relation to nouns.They function as determiners or pronouns and include words like some any many much few little several several enough all both half most none several a lot of lots of plenty of a few a little a number of.Examples:“Many students attended the lecture.” (countable plural).“Much time has passed.” (uncountable).“Few people know the secret.” (small number).“All the cookies are gone.” (total quantity).“Some water spilled on the floor.” (unspecified amount).Quantifiers can be pre-modifiers in noun phrases or stand alone as pronouns:“I need more.” “She ate several.”
Numeral.Numeral or number words express exact quantity or order in noun phrases.Cardinal numerals indicate how many (one two three four first second third) while ordinal numerals indicate position or order (first second third fourth fifth).They function as determiners adjectives or pronouns.Examples:“Three cats slept on the mat.” (cardinal determiner).“She finished second in the race.” (ordinal adjective).“The first book was better than the second.” (ordinal pronouns).“We need forty volunteers.” (cardinal).Numerals can combine with other determiners:“the first two days” “my three brothers” and follow specific order rules in noun phrases.
Particle. A particle is an invariable word that has no independent meaning but combines with verbs to form phrasal verbs multi-word verbs or verb-particle constructions changing or extending the verb’s meaning.Common particles include up down in out on off away back over through along across round apart aside.Examples:“She turned off the light.” (phrasal verb).“He ran away from home.” (directional particle).“They looked up the word.” (particle + preposition-like).“The plane took off late.” (particle verb).Particles can be separable (“pick it up”) or inseparable (“look after her”).Particles also appear in idiomatic expressions and adverbial uses.
Auxiliary Verb. An auxiliary verb also called helping verb assists the main verb to form tenses moods voices aspects questions negatives or passives.The primary auxiliaries are be have do while modal auxiliaries include can could may might will would shall should must.Examples:“She is running.” (be for progressive).“They have finished.” (have for perfect).“Do you know him?” (do for question/negation/emphasis).“He can swim.” (modal ability).“The book was read by everyone.” (be for passive).Auxiliaries do not carry main lexical meaning but provide grammatical information and invert for questions or take not for negation.
Modal Auxiliary. A modal auxiliary is a special type of auxiliary verb that expresses modality such as possibility ability permission obligation necessity advice prediction or volition.Core modals are can could may might will would shall should must while semi-modals include ought to used to dare need.Examples:“You must obey the rules.” (obligation).“It might rain later.” (possibility).“May I leave early?” (permission).“She can speak French.” (ability).“You should see a doctor.” (advice).Modals are defective: they do not inflect for person/number take no to-infinitive form no -s in third person and use bare infinitive after them.
Copula. A copula or linking verb connects the subject to a subject complement (adjective noun or phrase) describing or identifying the subject rather than expressing action.The main copula is be while others include seem appear become remain feel look sound taste smell grow turn get prove stay go run.Examples:“She is happy.” (be + adjective).“He became a doctor.” (become + noun).“The soup tastes delicious.” (taste + adjective).“It seems reasonable.” (seem + adjective).Copulas do not take objects and often allow predicative adjectives or noun phrases as complements.Some verbs shift between copular and action meanings.
Complement. A complement is a word phrase or clause that completes the meaning of a verb subject or object typically following a linking verb or certain other verbs.Complements include subject complements (after copulas like be seem become: “She is a teacher.” “He seems happy.”) and object complements (after verbs like call make find paint: “They elected her president.” “We painted the wall blue.”).Complements can be noun phrases adjective phrases prepositional phrases or clauses.Examples:“The soup tastes too salty.” (adjective complement).“His dream became reality.” (noun complement).“They consider him unreliable.” (adjective object complement).“I found the task difficult.” (adjective).Complements are required to make the clause grammatically and semantically complete unlike optional adverbials.
Object. A direct object is a noun phrase or pronoun that receives the action of a transitive verb answering “what?” or “whom?” after the verb.An indirect object receives the direct object and usually answers “to whom?” or “for whom?” appearing before the direct object or after with a preposition.Examples:“She kicked the ball.” (direct object ball).“He gave her a gift.” (indirect her direct gift).“They sent the letter to their friend.” (prepositional indirect).“We painted the house red.” (direct house object complement red).Objects can be noun clauses: “I know what happened.” (direct clause).Transitive verbs require direct objects while ditransitive verbs take both.
Subject. A subject is the noun phrase pronoun or clause that performs the action of the verb or about which something is stated in a clause.The subject typically precedes the verb in declarative sentences and agrees with it in person and number.Examples:“The dog barked loudly.” (noun phrase subject).“She runs every morning.” (pronoun subject).“Swimming is fun.” (gerund subject).“What he said surprised me.” (noun clause subject).“There are many books.” (existential there construction dummy subject).Subjects can be compound (“Tom and Jerry fought.”) or understood in imperatives (“Close the door.” implying you).
Predicate. The predicate is the part of a clause that says something about the subject consisting of the verb and any objects complements adverbials or other modifiers.The predicate expresses the action state or relation involving the subject.Examples:“The children played in the park.” (played in the park predicate).“She became a famous author.” (became a famous author predicate).“He knows the answer.” (knows the answer predicate).“Birds fly.” (fly simple predicate).The simple predicate is just the verb while the complete predicate includes all additional elements.
Modifier. A modifier is a word phrase or clause that provides additional information about another word or group changing or restricting its meaning.Modifiers include adjectives (modify nouns/pronouns) adverbs (modify verbs adjectives adverbs) adjective phrases adverb phrases prepositional phrases participial phrases relative clauses and more.Examples:“The very tall building collapsed.” (very tall adjective phrase modifier).“She spoke quite softly.” (quite softly adverb phrase).“The man who called left a message.” (relative clause modifier).Modifiers can be restrictive (essential no commas) or non-restrictive (additional commas).
Appositive. An appositive is a noun or noun phrase placed next to another noun to rename explain or identify it usually set off by commas for non-restrictive appositives or without commas for restrictive ones.Appositives provide extra detail or clarification.Examples:“My sister the doctor lives in Toronto.” (non-restrictive appositive).“The planet Mars is red.” (restrictive no commas).“Einstein the physicist developed relativity.” (non-restrictive).“Her cat Whiskers is playful.” (restrictive).Appositives can be reduced from relative clauses and often function like non-restrictive relative clauses.
Adverbial. A word phrase or clause that functions as an adverb modifying a verb adjective another adverb or entire sentence typically indicating manner place time frequency degree reason purpose condition or concession.Examples:“She spoke softly.” (manner adverb).“We will meet tomorrow.” (time adverb).“He works here.” (place adverb).“Quite surprisingly the plan succeeded.” (degree sentence adverb).Adverbials can be single words (“quickly”) prepositional phrases (“with care”) adverb phrases (“very carefully”) or adverbial clauses (“because it rained”).They are optional but add crucial detail to predicates.
Attributive. An attributive adjective or modifier is one that appears directly before the noun it modifies within the noun phrase rather than after a linking verb.Attributive position is the default for most descriptive adjectives.Examples:“A beautiful garden” (beautiful attributive).“The red car” (red attributive).“Her new Italian shoes” (new Italian attributive).“An extremely difficult problem” (extremely difficult attributive phrase).Attributive adjectives usually cannot take complements (“*fond sweets girl” incorrect) though some exceptions exist in compounds (“a duty-free shop”).
Predicative. A predicative adjective or complement appears after a linking verb (copula) and describes or identifies the subject rather than preceding the noun.Predicative position is common after be seem become appear feel look sound taste etc.Examples:“The sky is blue.” (blue predicative).“She seems happy.” (happy predicative).“The soup tastes delicious.” (delicious predicative).“He became president.” (president predicative noun).Predicative adjectives can often take complements (“She is fond of music.”) and allow adverb modification more freely than attributive ones.
Postpositive. A postpositive modifier (usually an adjective) follows the noun it modifies rather than preceding it.Postpositive adjectives are less common in English but occur in fixed expressions certain adjectives or poetic/literary styles.Examples:“The president elect” (elect postpositive).“Court martial” (martial postpositive).“The people involved” (involved postpositive).“A battle royal” (royal postpositive).“Something strange” (strange postpositive indefinite pronoun).“Heir apparent” (apparent postpositive).Postpositive position often occurs with indefinite pronouns (anything available) or in French-influenced expressions.
Intensifier. An intensifier is a word (usually an adverb) that strengthens or weakens the meaning of an adjective another adverb or sometimes a verb.Intensifiers include very extremely quite rather fairly pretty somewhat a bit a little absolutely totally completely utterly barely scarcely hardly.Examples:“Very happy” (very intensifies happy).“Quite slowly” (quite intensifies slowly).“Absolutely ridiculous” (absolutely strong intensifier).“Somewhat tired” (somewhat weakens tired).“Pretty good” (pretty informal intensifier).Intensifiers can be degree adverbs and some change meaning based on gradable vs non-gradable adjectives.
Subordinator. A subordinator is a word that introduces a subordinate (dependent) clause linking it to the main clause and indicating the grammatical relationship.Subordinators include subordinating conjunctions (because although if when since while unless until whereas) relative pronouns (who whom whose which that) and other markers (that whether how why).Examples:“Because it rained we stayed home.” (because subordinator).“The book that I read” (that relative subordinator).“I wonder whether she will come.” (whether subordinator).Subordinators make clauses dependent preventing them from standing alone as sentences.
Coordinator. A coordinator is a conjunction that joins elements of equal grammatical rank such as words phrases or independent clauses.Coordinating conjunctions include and but or nor for so yet (FANBOYS).Examples:“Tea and coffee” (and coordinator words).“She was tired but happy.” (but coordinator clauses).“You can stay or leave.” (or coordinator).“It rained so we canceled.” (so coordinator result).Coordinators create compound structures and do not make one element subordinate to the other.
Complementizer. A complementizer is a word or morpheme that introduces a complement clause typically a noun clause or reported clause making it function as the complement of a verb adjective or noun.The most common complementizer in English is that though it is often omitted in informal speech.Other complementizers include if whether how why that.Examples:“I think that she is right.” (that complementizer).“I wonder if he will come.” (if complementizer).“She asked whether it was true.” (whether complementizer).“Tell me how you did it.” (how complementizer).“The fact that it rained surprised no one.” (that introducing noun clause).Complementizers turn clauses into nominal elements and are optional in many cases with verbs like say believe know think.
Dummy Subject. A dummy subject is a pronoun or word (usually it or there) that fills the subject position in a clause but carries no semantic meaning referring to no actual entity.It serves a grammatical function to satisfy the requirement for an overt subject in English.Examples:“It is raining.” (dummy it weather).“There are many people here.” (dummy there existential).“It seems that he left early.” (dummy it extraposition).“It took three hours to finish.” (dummy it with infinitive).“There exists a solution.” (dummy there).Dummy subjects allow certain constructions (existentials weather clefts extraposition) that would otherwise lack a subject.
Extraposition. Extraposition is a syntactic process where a heavy or complex element (usually a clause) is moved to the end of the sentence with a dummy subject (it) placed in its original position to improve readability and balance.Extraposition is common with subject clauses object clauses or adjective complements.Examples:“It surprised me that she arrived late.” (extraposed that-clause original: That she arrived late surprised me).“It is obvious why he quit.” (extraposed why-clause).“It worries me how little time we have.” (extraposed how-clause).“It was kind of you to help.” (extraposed infinitive).Extraposition avoids heavy subjects at the beginning and follows end-weight principles in English.
Fronting. Fronting is the placement of a normally non-initial element (adverb complement object phrase) at the beginning of a clause for emphasis contrast topic marking or stylistic effect often with subject-verb inversion in some cases.Examples:“Never have I seen such beauty.” (fronted negative adverb inversion).“This book I really enjoyed.” (fronted object no inversion).“In the garden the children played.” (fronted adverbial).“Happy is the man who helps others.” (fronted complement inversion).“Only then did he understand.” (fronted only then inversion).Fronting highlights the fronted element and is common in literary formal or emphatic speech.
Clefting. Clefting is a syntactic construction that divides a simple sentence into two clauses to place focus or emphasis on a particular element using it-clefts wh-clefts (pseudo-clefts) or reversed wh-clefts.Examples:“It was the dog that barked.” (it-cleft focusing dog).“What I need is coffee.” (wh-cleft focusing coffee).“Coffee is what I need.” (reversed wh-cleft).“It was yesterday that we met.” (it-cleft focusing time).“The one who called was John.” (it-cleft variant).Cleft sentences highlight new important or contrastive information and restructure for better discourse flow.
Inversion. Inversion is the reversal of the usual subject-verb order in a clause often for emphasis question formation conditional without if after negative adverbs or in literary styles.Examples:“Is she coming?” (question inversion).“Never have I felt so alive.” (negative adverb inversion).“Had I known I would have acted.” (conditional inversion).“Here comes the bus.” (adverbial inversion).“Said the witch ‘Never return!’” (direct speech inversion).Inversion adds stylistic variety emphasis or grammatical necessity in specific contexts.
Ellipsis. Ellipsis is the omission of words that are understood from context usually to avoid repetition or for conciseness. Ellipsis occurs in coordinated structures comparatives responses and informal speech. Examples: “She likes tea and he coffee. ” (elliptical: likes omitted). “He can run faster than I. ” (elliptical: can run omitted). “When possible I’ll call. ” (elliptical: it is omitted). “Some prefer red others blue. ” (elliptical: prefer omitted). “If necessary do it yourself. ” (elliptical: it is omitted). Ellipsis maintains parallelism and brevity while remaining grammatically clear.
Anaphora. Anaphora is the use of a word or phrase (usually a pronoun) that refers back to a previously mentioned word phrase or idea called the antecedent. Anaphora creates cohesion by linking sentences. Examples: “John arrived late. He was tired. ” (he anaphoric to John). “The book was interesting. I read it twice. ” (it anaphoric to the book). “She promised to help and she did. ” (she anaphoric). “The team won. Their victory was celebrated. ” (their anaphoric to the team). Anaphora avoids repetition and connects discourse logically.
Cataphora. Cataphora is the use of a word or phrase (often a pronoun) that refers forward to a word phrase or idea mentioned later in the text or discourse. Cataphora is less common than anaphora but creates anticipation. Examples: “When he arrived John was surprised. ” (he cataphoric to John). “If you see her tell Mary I called. ” (her cataphoric to Mary). “It was shocking what happened next. ” (it cataphoric to what happened next). “Before they left the guests thanked the host. ” (they cataphoric to the guests). Cataphora is frequent in narrative or formal styles.
Expletive. An expletive is a word or phrase that fills a syntactic position without adding semantic content often for grammatical reasons. Common expletives include it and there in dummy subject constructions and emphatic there. Examples: “It is important to study. ” (dummy it). “There seems to be a problem. ” (dummy there). “It was John who called. ” (cleft it). “There goes the bus. ” (presentational there). Expletives satisfy English’s subject requirement but carry no referential meaning.
Parenthetical. A parenthetical element is a word phrase or clause inserted into a sentence providing additional information comment or explanation usually set off by commas dashes or parentheses. Parentheticals are grammatically non-essential. Examples: “The plan unfortunately failed. ” (unfortunately parenthetical). “My brother a talented musician plays guitar. ” (a talented musician parenthetical). “The results as expected were positive. ” (as expected parenthetical). “She said and I quote ‘Never again. ’” (and I quote parenthetical). Parentheticals add asides clarification or emphasis without disrupting the main clause.
Vocative. A vocative is a noun or noun phrase used to address or call someone directly often set off by commas and not grammatically part of the sentence structure. Vocatives indicate direct address. Examples: “John come here. ” (John vocative). “Ladies and gentlemen welcome. ” (ladies and gentlemen vocative). “Doctor can you help. ” (doctor vocative). “My dear friends thank you. ” (my dear friends vocative). Vocatives appear at the beginning middle or end of sentences and add personal directness.
Coordination. Coordination is the grammatical process of joining two or more elements of equal syntactic status using coordinators such as and but or nor for so yet. Coordination creates compound structures at word phrase or clause level. Examples: “Apples and oranges” (word coordination). “Running quickly or walking slowly” (phrase coordination). “She laughed and he cried” (clause coordination). “Not tea nor coffee” (nor coordination). Coordination maintains parallelism and balance between conjoined elements.
Subordination. Subordination is the grammatical process where one clause depends on another for its full meaning using subordinators to create complex sentences. The subordinate clause cannot stand alone. Examples: “Because it rained we stayed inside” (because subordinates the clause). “Although tired she continued” (although subordinates). “The man who called left” (who subordinates relative clause). Subordination establishes hierarchical relationships showing cause time condition concession etc.
Agreement. Agreement also called concord is the grammatical correspondence between words in person number gender or case typically between subject and verb or pronoun and antecedent. Examples: “The dog barks” (singular agreement). “The dogs bark” (plural agreement). “Everyone has their book” (singular they agreement informal). “She and I are going” (compound subject plural). Agreement ensures grammatical consistency across sentence elements.
Tense. Tense is a grammatical category that locates an action or state in time relative to the moment of speaking primarily past present or future in English. English has two morphological tenses present and past with future expressed periphrastically. Examples: “She walks” (present). “She walked” (past). “She will walk” (future). “She has walked” (present perfect). Tense combines with aspect to form complex time references.
Aspect. Aspect is a grammatical category that indicates the internal temporal structure or completion status of an action independent of tense. English aspects include simple progressive perfect and perfect progressive. Examples: “She walks” (simple). “She is walking” (progressive ongoing). “She has walked” (perfect completed relevance). “She has been walking” (perfect progressive duration). Aspect conveys how an event unfolds over time.
Mood. Mood is a grammatical category expressing the speaker’s attitude toward the action such as fact possibility command wish or condition. English moods include indicative imperative subjunctive and conditional. Examples: “She goes” (indicative fact). “Go now!” (imperative command). “If I were rich” (subjunctive unreal). “He might come” (conditional possibility). Mood appears through verb forms auxiliaries or clause structure.
Voice. Voice is a grammatical category indicating the relationship between the verb subject and object primarily active or passive. Active voice has the subject performing the action while passive has the subject receiving it. Examples: “The chef cooked the meal” (active). “The meal was cooked by the chef” (passive). “Mistakes were made” (agentless passive). Voice shifts focus from doer to receiver or action.
Case. Case is a grammatical category marking the syntactic function of nouns or pronouns primarily nominative genitive and accusative objective in English. Modern English shows case mainly in pronouns. Examples: “She saw him” (she nominative him accusative). “This is her book” (her genitive possessive). “Whom did you see?” (whom accusative). Case indicates subject object or possessive roles.
Gender. Gender in English grammar is a category of agreement primarily in third-person singular pronouns he she it they reflecting natural gender or generic reference. English has natural not grammatical gender. Examples: “The boy lost his toy” (he his masculine). “The girl found her doll” (she her feminine). “The dog wagged its tail” (it neuter). “Everyone lost their keys” (singular they gender-neutral). Gender agreement occurs in pronouns and possessive adjectives.
Number. Number is a grammatical category indicating singularity or plurality affecting nouns verbs pronouns and determiners. English distinguishes singular and plural usually with -s/-es or irregular forms. Examples: “One cat sleeps” (singular). “Three cats sleep” (plural). “The child plays” vs “The children play” (irregular). “This book” vs “These books” (demonstrative agreement). Number agreement is required between subjects and verbs.
Person. Person is a grammatical category distinguishing the speaker first person addressee second person and others third person affecting pronouns and verb agreement especially in present tense. Examples: “I walk” (first). “You walk” (second). “He walks” (third singular -s). “We walk” (first plural). “They walk” (third plural). Person affects subject-verb agreement and pronoun choice.
Finite Verb. A finite verb shows tense person and number agreement with the subject and can stand as the main verb in an independent clause. Finite verbs contrast with non-finite forms. Examples: “She runs” (present third singular finite). “They ran” (past finite). “He is coming” (is finite auxiliary). Finite verbs head main and subordinate clauses expressing complete predication.
Non-finite Verb. A non-finite verb does not show tense person or number agreement and cannot head an independent clause. Non-finite forms include infinitives gerunds and participles. Examples: “To run” (infinitive). “Running” (gerund). “Running quickly” (present participle). “Broken” (past participle). Non-finite verbs appear in phrases complements or reduced clauses.
Phrasal Verb. A phrasal verb is a verb combined with a particle usually an adverb or preposition forming a single semantic unit often idiomatic. Phrasal verbs can be separable or inseparable. Examples: “Give up” (quit inseparable). “Pick it up” (separable). “Look after” (care for inseparable). “Turn off the light” (separable). “Run into a friend” (meet by chance). Phrasal verbs are highly productive and common in everyday English.
Collocation. A collocation is a habitual co-occurrence of words that sound natural together due to convention rather than strict grammatical rule. Collocations include adjective-noun verb-object etc. Examples: “Make a decision” (not do). “Heavy rain” (not strong). “Commit a crime” (not make). “Blonde hair” (not yellow). Collocations improve fluency and idiomaticity in language use.
Idiom. An idiom is a fixed multi-word expression whose meaning cannot be deduced from the literal meanings of its parts. Idioms are often non-compositional. Examples: “Kick the bucket” (die). “Spill the beans” (reveal secret). “Piece of cake” (easy). “Under the weather” (unwell). “Break a leg” (good luck). Idioms add color expressivity and cultural flavor to speech.
Valency.Valency is the number and type of arguments a verb requires or allows to complete its meaning.Valency determines whether a verb is intransitive monovalent transitive bivalent or ditransitive trivalent.Examples:“Sleep” (valency 1 subject only intransitive).“Hit” (valency 2 subject + direct object).“Give” (valency 3 subject + indirect object + direct object).“Put” (valency 3 subject + object + location).Verbs can have optional arguments increasing effective valency in context.
Valency Reduction.Valency reduction is a process where a verb’s argument structure is simplified often through passivization reflexivization or impersonal constructions reducing the number of overtly expressed arguments.Examples:“The door opened” (reduction from transitive open something).“They kissed” (reciprocal reduction).“It rains” (impersonal zero valency).“Mistakes were made” (passive agent omission).Valency reduction shifts focus or omits participants for stylistic or grammatical reasons.
Valency Increasing.Valency increasing is a process that adds arguments to a verb’s structure through causativization applicativization or other morphological/syntactic devices.Examples:“The sun melted the ice” (causative from melt intransitive).“She fed the baby” (causative from eat).“They laughed him out of the room” (resultative valency increase).Valency-increasing devices create new transitive or ditransitive uses.
Argument. Argument is a noun phrase or clause required or permitted by a verb’s valency to complete its meaning.Arguments include subjects direct objects indirect objects and prepositional objects.Examples:“She gave him a book” (subject her him indirect a book direct).“He thinks that she left” (subject he that-clause argument).Arguments are obligatory for semantic completeness unlike adjuncts.
Adjunct. An adjunct is an optional element that adds extra information to a clause usually adverbial in nature without being required by the verb’s valency.Adjuncts include time place manner reason purpose frequency.Examples:“She runs quickly in the park every morning” (quickly manner in the park place every morning time adjuncts).Adjuncts can often be removed without making the sentence ungrammatical.
Thematic Role. Thematic role also called theta-role or semantic role is the semantic relationship a noun phrase bears to the verb such as agent patient theme experiencer goal source instrument location.Examples:“The chef sliced the tomato with a knife” (chef agent tomato patient knife instrument).“She received a gift from him” (she goal gift theme him source).Thematic roles are assigned by the verb according to its theta-grid.
Theta Criterion. The theta criterion is a principle stating that each argument must receive exactly one thematic role and each thematic role must be assigned to exactly one argument.This ensures proper matching between syntax and semantics.Examples:Violation would be *“The book read John” (two agents one verb).The theta criterion prevents over- or under-assignment of roles.
Theta Grid. A theta grid is the lexical specification of a verb listing the number and types of thematic roles it assigns to its arguments.Examples:“Give” <agent goal theme>.“Run” <agent>.“Break” <agent patient> or <patient> intransitive use.The theta grid determines possible syntactic frames for the verb.
Dative Alternation. Dative alternation is the variation where a ditransitive verb can express the indirect object either as a bare NP before the direct object or as a prepositional phrase with to/for after it.Examples:“She gave him the book” vs “She gave the book to him.”“He baked her a cake” vs “He baked a cake for her.”Not all verbs allow both patterns.
Locative Alternation. Locative alternation is the variation where certain verbs allow the locative argument to appear either as direct object with theme in PP or theme as direct object with locative in PP.Examples:“She sprayed paint on the wall” vs “She sprayed the wall with paint.”“They loaded hay onto the truck” vs “They loaded the truck with hay.”The alternation often changes focus or affectedness.
Causative Alternation. Causative alternation is the pattern where some intransitive verbs have a transitive causative counterpart often with zero derivation.Examples:“The ice melted” vs “The sun melted the ice.”“The door opened” vs “He opened the door.”“The vase broke” vs “She broke the vase.”Not all intransitives alternate causatively.
Anticausative. Anticausative is the intransitive use of a verb that describes a change of state without an external agent often morphologically marked or zero-derived opposite of causative.Examples:“The window broke” (anticausative).“The shirt tore easily” (anticausative).Anticausatives focus on the patient undergoing change spontaneously or internally caused.
Middle Voice. Middle voice or middle construction is a structure where the subject is affected by the action and no external agent is expressed often with a generic or facilitative reading.Examples:“These books sell well.” (middle sell).“The door opens easily.” (middle open).“This fabric washes beautifully.” (middle wash).Middles imply inherent properties enabling the action.
Reflexive Reflexive. A reflexive construction uses a reflexive pronoun to indicate that the subject and object refer to the same entity.Examples:“She saw herself in the mirror.” (true reflexive).“He cut himself shaving.” (direct reflexive).Reflexives can be emphatic intensive or logophoric in some contexts.
Reciprocal. Reciprocal construction indicates mutual action between two or more participants often using each other or one another.Examples:“They love each other.” (reciprocal).“The two teams congratulated one another.” (reciprocal).Reciprocals reduce valency by combining two arguments into one.
Impersonal Construction. Impersonal construction is a clause with no referential subject using dummy it or there or zero subject in some languages to express weather time existence or general statements.Examples:“It’s snowing.” (weather impersonal).“It seems likely.” (modal impersonal).“There happened an accident.” (existential impersonal).
Raising. Raising is a construction where the subject or object of an embedded clause appears as the subject of the matrix clause with certain verbs like seem appear happen tend.Examples:“She seems to be happy.” (raising to subject from seems [she be happy]).“He is likely to win.” (raising).Raising verbs do not assign a thematic role to their surface subject.
Control. Control is a construction where the understood subject of a non-finite complement clause is controlled by an argument of the matrix verb.Examples:“She promised to help.” (promise controls PRO subject of to help).“He persuaded her to leave.” (persuade object controls PRO).Control verbs include promise try want ask persuade.
PRO. PRO is the null pronominal subject of non-finite clauses in control and some raising constructions not pronounced but interpreted via control or arbitrary reference.Examples:“[PRO to err] is human.” (arbitrary PRO).“She tried [PRO to swim].” (controlled PRO = she).PRO is phonologically empty but syntactically present.
Big PRO. Big PRO refers to the arbitrary or generic null subject in infinitival clauses without a controller.Examples:“It is fun [PRO to swim].” (arbitrary big PRO anyone).Big PRO contrasts with controlled small pro in obligatory control structures.
Small Clause. A small clause is a verbless clause consisting of a subject and predicate usually an adjective noun or prepositional phrase functioning as complement to a verb.Examples:“They consider him smart.” (him smart small clause).“She painted the wall red.” (wall red small clause).“We found the city in chaos.” (city in chaos small clause).Small clauses lack tense and finite verb.
